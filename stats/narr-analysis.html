Exploring Housing Challenges in Long Beach: Unraveling the Rent Burden Puzzle

In this exploration of our city's complex housing situation, our focus is on a significant issue that resonates with many residents: Rent Burden. Imagine spending a substantial chunk of your hard-earned income just to keep a roof over your head. That's the reality for a large number of households in Long Beach.

What is rent burden?

Rent burden occurs when a household spends 30% or more of their income on housing costs. This is the threshold established by HUD. When families cross this line, they have less money for essentials like food, healthcare, transportation, and education. Those spending 50% or more are considered severely cost burdened — one financial emergency away from potential homelessness.

The citywide picture: a decade of data

Using 10 years of American Community Survey data (from 2010-2014 through 2020-2024), we can track how rent burden has evolved across Long Beach's nine council districts.

Citywide, 53.1% of renter households are cost burdened — approximately 53,104 households. While this represents a modest improvement from 55.4% a decade ago, the total number of burdened households has actually grown because Long Beach added more than 12,702 renter households over this period.

Key finding: The overall burden rate improved slightly, but the number of people affected grew. More renters are in Long Beach today, and more of them are cost burdened than a decade ago.

The senior crisis: a growing population under pressure

Perhaps the most concerning trend is among senior renters aged 65 and over. The 65+ renter population grew by 39.7% over the decade — from approximately 8,702 to 12,153 households. Their cost burden rate has held stubbornly high at 63.7%, meaning the number of burdened seniors has surged by over 2,234.

Seniors living on fixed incomes have few options when rents rise. They can't simply work more hours or switch careers. For many, the choice becomes paying rent or paying for medications, food, or other necessities.

The displacement signal: low-income households disappearing

The data on low-income renter households (those earning less than $35,000/year) tells a troubling story of displacement. Over the past decade, the number of low-income renter households dropped by 15,165 — from 41,082 to 25,917.

These households didn't all get wealthier. While some crossed the $35,000 threshold due to wage growth and inflation, a significant portion were displaced from Long Beach entirely — pushed out by rising rents to more affordable areas further from their jobs, schools, and communities.

The displacement paradox: Among the low-income renters who remain, conditions are worse. The severe burden rate climbed from 59.5% to 70.8%. Fewer low-income households, but those still here are under greater financial strain than ever.

What varies by district?

Housing burden is not evenly distributed across Long Beach. Districts 6, 1, and 2 consistently show the highest burden rates, while Districts 3 and 4 are lower. These patterns often correlate with historical patterns of disinvestment, housing stock age, and proximity to employment centers.

Why this matters

Housing is the foundation of community stability. When families spend too much on rent, neighborhoods lose their long-term residents, children change schools mid-year, and local businesses lose customers. The data on this page isn't just numbers — it represents real families making impossible choices between rent and groceries, between staying in their community and finding somewhere they can afford.

By tracking these indicators over time and across districts, LiBRE provides the evidence base that advocates, policymakers, and community members need to push for meaningful change: stronger renter protections, more affordable housing production, and targeted assistance for the most vulnerable — especially our seniors and lowest-income neighbors.
